What you are looking at

Any reasonable periodic function can be written as an infinite sum of sines and cosines — its Fourier series. Each circle on the left is one term of that sum: a vector of length \( |a_k| \) rotating at angular frequency \( k \). The vectors are chained tip to tail, so the height of the final tip is exactly the partial sum $$ f_n(t) = a_0 + \sum_{k=1}^{n} a_k \sin(\omega_k t + \varphi_k), $$ which the blue curve records as time flows. The dashed curve is the exact target waveform; the readout shows the root-mean-square error of the approximation.

The series being built

The four target waves and their series (only non-zero terms are drawn as circles):

Square — odd harmonics decaying like \( 1/k \): $$ f(t) = \frac{4}{\pi} \sum_{k \text{ odd}} \frac{\sin(kt)}{k}. $$

Sawtooth — every harmonic, alternating sign: $$ f(t) = \frac{2}{\pi} \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} (-1)^{k+1} \frac{\sin(kt)}{k}. $$

Triangle — odd harmonics decaying like \( 1/k^2 \), which is why so few terms already fit it almost perfectly: $$ f(t) = \frac{8}{\pi^2} \sum_{k \text{ odd}} (-1)^{\frac{k-1}{2}} \frac{\sin(kt)}{k^2}. $$

Pulse — a 25% duty cycle needs a constant term and cosine harmonics, some of which vanish: $$ f(t) = (2d - 1) + \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{4}{k\pi} \sin(k\pi d)\, \cos(kt), \qquad d = \tfrac{1}{4}. $$

Things to try

  • Slide the harmonic count up on the square wave and watch the overshoot near each jump refuse to shrink below about 9% — the Gibbs phenomenon. More terms squeeze the overshoot narrower, never shorter.
  • Compare how fast the triangle converges against the square: coefficients decaying like \( 1/k^2 \) instead of \( 1/k \) is the smoothness of the target showing up in the frequency domain.
  • On the pulse, notice every fourth circle is missing — \( \sin(k\pi/4) \) vanishes when \( k \) is a multiple of 4.
  • Turn off the epicycles and watch just the wave: the RMS error in the readout is the quantity the truncated series minimizes.

Fast algorithms for computing these coefficients are the subject of Computational Cost and FFT, which looks at the discrete Fourier transform inside convolutional networks.
