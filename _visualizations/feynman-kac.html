What you are looking at

A cloud of random walkers starts at the marked point \( x_0 \) and diffuses through the domain \( D \) until each one either reaches the boundary, gets killed, or wanders too long. The Feynman–Kac formula says that the average of what those walkers experience is exactly the solution of a partial differential equation: $$ \tfrac{\sigma^2}{2}\,\Delta u \;+\; b\cdot\nabla u \;-\; c\,u \;+\; f \;=\; 0 \quad\text{in } D, \qquad u = g \;\text{ on } \partial D, $$ where the walkers follow the stochastic differential equation \( \mathrm{d}X_t = b(X_t)\,\mathrm{d}t + \sigma\,\mathrm{d}W_t \) and \( \tau = \inf\{t : X_t \notin D\} \) is the first exit time. The probabilistic representation is $$ u(x_0) \;=\; \mathbb{E}_{x_0}\!\left[ e^{-c\tau}\, g(X_\tau) \;+\; \int_0^\tau e^{-c s}\, f(X_s)\,\mathrm{d}s \right]. $$ The background shading is the same \( u \) computed by a finite-difference solve of the PDE (or by a closed formula when one exists), so you can watch the Monte-Carlo average and the analytic solution agree. In the 3D view the walkers move on the graph of \( u \) itself — a random walk exploring its own solution surface.

The dictionary

Every term of the equation is a knob in the panel, and each one is a process:

  • \( \sigma \) (diffusion) — the walkers' jitter. Doubling \( \sigma \) quarters mean exit times: time scales like \( 1/\sigma^2 \).
  • \( b \) (drift) — a velocity field pushing the walkers (the arrows). A constant wind skews the harmonic measure toward the downwind boundary; the restoring well traps walkers and stretches \( \tau \); the vortex stirs without changing how far from the center a driftless walker would settle.
  • \( c \) (killing rate) — each walker carries an exponential clock and dies with rate \( c \) (the little ×). Death before the boundary means payoff zero — which is exactly the discount factor \( e^{-c\tau} \) in expectation. This is the probabilistic meaning of the zeroth-order term \( -cu \).
  • \( f \) (running reward) — accrued continuously while alive. With \( f \equiv 1 \) and no payoff, \( u(x_0) = \mathbb{E}[\tau] \): the mean exit time solves \( \tfrac{\sigma^2}{2}\Delta u = -1 \), the torsion function.
  • \( g \) (boundary payoff) — 1 on the green target set \( \Gamma \), 0 on the rest. With everything else off, \( u(x_0) = \mathbb{P}(X_\tau \in \Gamma) \) is the harmonic measure of \( \Gamma \): exit probabilities are harmonic functions.

Domains where the answer is a formula

Three of the domains have classical closed forms (used as the dashed reference), all for the driftless, killing-free walk:

  • Disk — harmonic measure is the integral of the Poisson kernel \( P(x,\theta) = \frac{1-|x|^2}{2\pi\,|x - e^{i\theta}|^2} \) over the target arc; pick the “Exit directions” chart to see the histogram of exit angles converge to this kernel. The mean exit time is the beautiful \( \mathbb{E}_x[\tau] = (1-|x|^2)/(2\sigma^2) \).
  • Annulus — the 2D gambler's ruin: the probability of reaching the outer circle before the inner one from radius \( \rho \) is \( \ln(\rho/r)/\ln(R/r) \), because \( \ln|x| \) is harmonic in the plane.
  • Rectangle — separation of variables gives sine–sinh series for both the exit probability and the torsion function.
  • Sector, L-shape, flower — no closed form (the reentrant corners ruin it); here the finite-difference solver is the only reference, and Monte Carlo works exactly the same. That indifference to geometry is the whole point of the method.

The parabolic connection

The same walkers answer time-dependent questions. The function \( v(t,x) = \mathbb{P}_x(\tau > t) \) — the probability of still being inside at time \( t \) — solves the heat equation \( \partial_t v = \tfrac{\sigma^2}{2}\Delta v \) with absorbing boundary \( v = 0 \) on \( \partial D \) and \( v(0,\cdot) = 1 \). Expanding in Dirichlet eigenfunctions, \( v(t,x) = \sum_k a_k \varphi_k(x)\, e^{-\lambda_k t} \), so for large \( t \) the survival probability decays like \( e^{-\lambda_1 t} \): the principal eigenvalue is the asymptotic rate at which Brownian motion leaks out of the domain. Choose the “Survival \( P(\tau > t) \)” chart: on a log scale the empirical survival curve becomes a straight line, and on the disk its fitted slope converges to the exact \( \lambda_1 = \sigma^2 j_{0,1}^2/2 \), with \( j_{0,1} \approx 2.405 \) the first zero of the Bessel function \( J_0 \). The elliptic problems above are just this parabolic picture integrated over all time — the Feynman–Kac formula for \( \partial_t u + \tfrac{\sigma^2}{2}\Delta u + b\cdot\nabla u - cu + f = 0 \) reads the same way with a finite horizon.

Why averaging walkers solves a PDE

The one-line reason: for \( M_t = e^{-ct}\,u(X_t) + \int_0^t e^{-cs} f(X_s)\,\mathrm{d}s \), Itô's formula gives \( \mathrm{d}M_t = e^{-ct}\big(\mathcal{L}u - cu + f\big)\mathrm{d}t + (\text{martingale}) \), where \( \mathcal{L} = \tfrac{\sigma^2}{2}\Delta + b\cdot\nabla \) is the generator. If \( u \) solves the PDE, the drift term vanishes, \( M \) is a martingale, and optional stopping at \( \tau \) yields the representation. Run it in reverse and Monte Carlo constructs \( u \) with no grid, no stencil, and no fear of weird geometry — which is why this identity powers everything from option pricing to neutron transport.

Things to try

  • On the disk, drag \( x_0 \) toward the boundary and watch the exit-direction histogram sharpen into a spike — the Poisson kernel degenerating toward a point mass at the nearest boundary point.
  • Switch to Mean exit time on the disk: the 3D surface is the paraboloid \( (1-|x|^2)/(2\sigma^2) \). Halve \( \sigma \) and watch the whole dome quadruple.
  • On the annulus, put \( x_0 \) at the geometric mean radius \( \sqrt{rR} \approx 0.63 \) — gambler's ruin says the exit probability is exactly \( \tfrac12 \).
  • Turn on a strong wind and watch the CI band: drift toward \( \Gamma \) makes hits common and the estimate settles fast; drift away makes hits rare and the Monte-Carlo error visibly heavier — a variance-reduction lesson in one slider.
  • Raise the killing rate with the Laplace preset: \( u(x_0) = \mathbb{E}[e^{-c\tau}; X_\tau \in \Gamma] \) is the Laplace transform of the hitting time — the object behind every discounted price in finance.
  • Compare survival tails: the flower's tips drain probability faster than the L-shape's deep corner — domains with the same area can have very different \( \lambda_1 \).
  • Crank \( \Delta t \) to its maximum and watch the estimate drift below the reference: discrete walkers overshoot the boundary, a real bias of the Euler–Maruyama scheme.
  • Watch the very first seconds after a reset: the streaming estimate starts biased, because only finished walkers are counted and quick exits finish first — a live demonstration of size-biased sampling. The “fast” button runs each walker to completion, so its samples are clean draws.
