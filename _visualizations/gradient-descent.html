What you are looking at

The surface is the graph of a scalar field \( f(x, y) \) — a loss landscape. Click anywhere on it to drop one particle per active optimizer at that point; every particle then follows its own update rule downhill. The trails show the paths taken, the ring marks the global minimum (when the landscape has a unique, known one), and the chart below records the loss \( f(x_k, y_k) \) at every iteration \( k \), so you can compare convergence speed directly. The 2D view shows the same landscape as a contour map, which often makes oscillation and zig-zagging easier to see.

The update rules

Writing \( g_k = \nabla f(x_k) \) for the gradient at the current iterate, the optimizers implement:

Gradient descent takes a plain step along the negative gradient: $$ x_{k+1} = x_k - \alpha\, g_k. $$

Momentum accumulates a velocity that smooths the trajectory and accelerates along persistent directions: $$ v_{k+1} = \beta v_k - \alpha\, g_k, \qquad x_{k+1} = x_k + v_{k+1}. $$

Nesterov evaluates the gradient at the look-ahead point \( x_k + \beta v_k \) instead, which corrects the velocity before overshooting: $$ v_{k+1} = \beta v_k - \alpha\, \nabla f(x_k + \beta v_k), \qquad x_{k+1} = x_k + v_{k+1}. $$

RMSProp divides each coordinate of the step by a running estimate of the gradient's magnitude, adapting the step size per direction: $$ s_{k+1} = \rho s_k + (1-\rho)\, g_k^2, \qquad x_{k+1} = x_k - \frac{\alpha\, g_k}{\sqrt{s_{k+1}} + \varepsilon}. $$

Adam combines both ideas — a momentum-like first moment and an RMSProp-like second moment, each bias-corrected: $$ m_{k+1} = \beta_1 m_k + (1-\beta_1) g_k, \qquad s_{k+1} = \beta_2 s_k + (1-\beta_2) g_k^2, $$ $$ x_{k+1} = x_k - \alpha\, \frac{\hat m_{k+1}}{\sqrt{\hat s_{k+1}} + \varepsilon}, \qquad \hat m = \frac{m}{1-\beta_1^{k}},\; \hat s = \frac{s}{1-\beta_2^{k}}. $$

Gravity is not an optimizer at all: it integrates a ball rolling on the surface under gravity \( g \) with friction \( \mu \) (semi-implicit Euler). It is a useful physical intuition pump — compare how its inertia differs from momentum's.

Things to try

  • On Rosenbrock, watch vanilla gradient descent crawl along the curved valley while Adam adapts its per-coordinate step.
  • On Rastrigin or Egg Crate, drop particles far from the center: momentum methods can roll through shallow local minima that trap vanilla descent.
  • On the Saddle, see every method escape along the unstable direction — and how long that takes from a nearly balanced start.
  • Push \( \beta \) close to 1 on Quadratic Bowl to make momentum orbit the minimum before settling.

About the toolkit

This page runs on a small, dependency-free visualization toolkit written for this site: a canvas 3D surface renderer, a contour/heatmap view and a line-chart primitive, driven by a shared registry, theme bridge and control-panel builder. New landscapes and optimizers are single self-contained entries in a registry, and new tools mount from a single data-viz attribute — the aim is to make any future scientific or mathematical idea on this site explorable, not just this one.
