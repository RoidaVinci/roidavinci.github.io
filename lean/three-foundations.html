--- layout: lean title: "One statement, three foundations" description: "The dot product on R^n written in set theory, simple type theory, and dependent type theory." permalink: /lean/three-foundations/ math: true ---

Part 1 used dependent types without naming them. To see what they contribute, take one small statement and write it in three foundations:

\[ u \cdot v \;=\; \sum_{i} u_i v_i \qquad (u, v \in \mathbb{R}^n). \]

The statement is chosen for the letter \(n\): the dimension is an ordinary number that wants to appear in the description of the space itself, and the three foundations accommodate that in three different ways. (Comparing systems on one fixed statement follows Freek Wiedijk’s The Seventeen Provers of the World, which collected the irrationality of \(\sqrt2\) across seventeen systems.) In each case, consider how the foundation expresses \(\mathbb{R}^n\), the product \(u \cdot v\), and — the revealing case — what \(u \cdot v\) means when \(u \in \mathbb{R}^3\), \(v \in \mathbb{R}^5\).

Everything is a set

ZFC has sets, the relation \(\in\), and first-order logic — nothing else. With \(n = \{0, 1, \dots, n-1\}\) (von Neumann),

\[ \mathbb{R}^n := \{\, u \subseteq n \times \mathbb{R} : u \text{ functional and total} \,\}, \qquad u \cdot v := \textstyle\sum_{i \in n} u(i)\, v(i), \]

where a function is a set of ordered pairs and a pair \((a,b)\) is the set \(\{\, \{a\}, \{a,b\} \,\}\) (Kuratowski). The set-theoretic prover Mizar proceeds this way: its EUCLID theory defines REAL n as the \(n\)-tuples on \(\mathbb{R}\), finite sequences with domain \(\{1,\dots,n\}\).

The dimension lives in a proposition. “\(u \in \mathbb{R}^n\)” is a statement on the same footing as “\(u\) is continuous” — assumed, proved, carried through arguments by hand. No grammar catches mistakes, because every well-formed formula about sets is grammatical: \(u \cdot v\) for mismatched \(u, v\) simply denotes some junk object, and even “\(3 \in \pi\)” is a legitimate question (true if \(\mathbb{R}\) is built from Dedekind cuts, false if from Cauchy sequences). Kevin Buzzard calls these junk questions: harmless individually, but the formal language and the mathematician’s sense of what is sayable disagree.

In exchange: complete flexibility. \(\mathbb{R}^{m+n}\) and \(\mathbb{R}^{n+m}\) are the same set, unions of arbitrary structures are sets like any other, and nothing separates kinds of objects. The checker helps with nothing; everything rests on the proof obligations.

Types, but no numbers inside them

Simple type theory (Church, 1940), the foundation of HOL Light and Isabelle/HOL, gives every expression a type and rejects ill-typed expressions as ungrammatical. But types are built only from base types and type variables by \(\to\) and products: a term, such as \(n\), can never occur inside a type, so “\(\mathbb{R}^n\) for variable \(n\)” is not a type. Two workarounds are standard.

Lists with side conditions. Work in the type of finite real sequences and carry \(\operatorname{length} u = n\) as a hypothesis in every theorem — the dimension back in a proposition, as in set theory.

The Harrison encoding. In “A HOL theory of Euclidean space” (2005), John Harrison writes real^N for the function space \(N \to \mathbb{R}\) where \(N\) is a type variable, and the dimension is the cardinality of \(N\) — in his words, encoding the dimension “in a simple and useful way, even though HOL does not permit dependent types.” A theorem about real^N instantiates at any 3-element type to give \(\mathbb{R}^3\), and a mismatched product becomes a type error. Isabelle’s analysis library uses this encoding ('a^'b, due to Amine Chaieb). The seams show exactly where the dimension must behave like a number: concatenation needs a purpose-built type constructor (real^(M,N)finite_sum) with a theorem that its size is the sum of the sizes; “for every \(n\)” and “there exists \(n\)” over dimensions are not statements of the logic but schemas instantiated from outside; and, Harrison in the 2013 journal version: “proofs by induction over dimension are usually done in a somewhat intricate style … since we cannot directly perform induction on the size of types.”

In exchange: a simple type discipline with fast, decidable checking — a genuine advantage, defended in Larry Paulson’s “Formalising Mathematics in Simple Type Theory” and weighed on the next page.

Types may contain numbers

A dependent type contains an ordinary mathematical object. In Lean, Fin n is the type \(\{0, \dots, n-1\}\), depending on n : ℕ, and \(\mathbb{R}^n\) is Fin n → ℝ:

def dot {n : ℕ} (u v : Fin n → ℝ) : ℝ := ∑ i, u i * v i

The dimension lives in the type. The number \(n\) — an implicit argument, as in Part 1 — occurs inside the types of u and v, forcing both to mention the same \(n\). A mismatch is not a false statement or a forgotten side condition; it fails to parse as mathematics:

example (u : Fin 3 → ℝ) (v : Fin 5 → ℝ) : ℝ := dot u v
-- error: Application type mismatch: The argument v has type
--   Fin 5 → ℝ   but is expected to have type   Fin 3 → ℝ

And since the dimension is an honest number, no further machinery is needed: concatenation has type (Fin m → ℝ) → (Fin n → ℝ) → (Fin (m + n) → ℝ) — the statement about lengths is the type of the operation — and quantification or induction over the dimension is ordinary quantification and induction. Mathlib’s Euclidean space is exactly this with the \(L^2\) norm attached: EuclideanSpace ℝ (Fin n).

Part 1 already contained the general fact: \(\forall\) itself is a dependent type. One mechanism — terms inside types — yields the quantifiers, \(\mathbb{R}^n\), and propositions-as-types simultaneously. That economy is the argument of the next page.

The comparison in four questions

Where does “\(\dim u = \dim v\)” live?
ZFC: in a proposition, carried by hand. HOL: side condition, or enforced by the checker under the Harrison encoding. Lean: enforced by the checker.
Is “\(3 \in \pi\)” a legitimate question?
ZFC: yes (junk). HOL and Lean: no — ill-formed.
Can one quantify, or induct, over the dimension?
ZFC: freely. HOL: not inside the logic — schemas and “intricate” induction (Harrison). Lean: freely.
Are \(\mathbb{R}^{m+n}\) and \(\mathbb{R}^{n+m}\) the same object?
ZFC: the same set. HOL: distinct types with bespoke glue. Lean: distinct types, identified by transport along \(m+n = n+m\) — the cost examined in the fine print.

The last two questions summarize the whole tradeoff: the more mathematics is moved into the grammar, the more the checker verifies unaided — and the more the grammar itself becomes an object one must do mathematics to.
